Review of Haslemere Musical Society Concert Dec 6, 2014

Another full house gathered on 6th December to hear the Haslemere Musical Society with both chorus and orchestra in fine form, tackling two heaven-storming works from the turn of the nineteenth century that say much about the turbulence of their period: Haydn's Nelson Mass and Beethoven's Symphony no.5.

We began in a deceptively light mood, with the overture from Mozart's Cosi fan tutte. The orchestra played with a true chamber-like tone, with Mozart's quickfire detail passed seamlessly between flute, oboe and bassoon, and sharply etched syncopated rhythms.

Then for a complete switch in mood: to the ‘time of war' depicted in Haydn's Mass. The turbulent textures of the Kyrie happily did not prevent Jessica Broad's magnificent soprano from being heard above the full chorus and orchestra. Broad was there again at the very outset of the Gloria, now leading a genial song of praise. Throughout the Mass, the many switches in texture and dynamic were caught attentively by the orchestra and the chorus, and I was delighted to hear that the words were crystal clear (no small feat, as I can testify from many concerts spent positioned in the midst of the chorus). The excellence of the chorus's performance was a real testament to the work done over the past two years by the departing Chorus Master, Christopher Hann. Soloists, chorus and orchestra alike clearly responded to the sheer gratefulness of Haydn's writing - the music of a big-hearted individual, showing that even in times of turbulence and anger there is space for rejoicing.

After the interval came possibly the best-known opening in all music: Beethoven's depiction of Fate knocking at the door. The opening movement took a while to settle rhythmically, which was not surprising given the sheer number of pauses and discontinuities Beethoven makes the musicians deal with. The drama of these pauses was well caught by James Ross's direction. The second, not-exactly-slow movement was much happier in terms of ensemble, and kept an appropriate sense of nervous tension throughout. As the scherzo led implacably to the blazing major-key finale, I have to confess I was thrilled by the introduction of piccolo, three trombones and - yes! - contrabassoon, all of whom gave a weight of sound that was entirely appropriate to the triumphant conclusion, and yet never spoilt the flow of the music.

In a welcome nod to the season, we rounded off with a few carols with orchestral backing. The audience took the opportunity to join in - notably lustily. Perhaps, on the basis of this inspiring evening of music-making, a few can be persuaded to cross over to the performers' side in future? Thank you again, HMS, for all the pleasure you give the concert-goers of Haslemere and beyond.

Matthew O'Sullivan
