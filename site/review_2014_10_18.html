As the autumn nights start to draw in, it is always good to have a new season of Haslemere Musical Society concerts to lift the spirits. The first concert in the season, under the genial direction of James Ross, featured the HMS orchestra in music that ranged from the familiar to the all-but-forgotten.

To start with the rarity: HMS's principal oboist, David Farnworth, had unearthed the Oboe Concertino by Johann Kalliwoda. I found a lot to like in the piece, which starts in the same kind of world as his near-contemporary Schubert's military marches, but ranges widely in style as it proceeds. From the oboe's first entry, unexpectedly cutting the orchestra off in mid-flow, the soloist barely gets a break, and Farnworth looked distinctly relieved in the small moments of respite. Justifiably so: he navigated the reams of notes with virtuosity, while never losing sight of the ever-shifting character of the solo line. What a shame that works for oboe and orchestra are so few and far between in our concert halls, as in Farnworth's hands the oboe has a fascinatingly varied personality. The orchestra maintained focus throughout, even when the spotlight was firmly on the soloist.

At the end of the evening was Mozart's ‘Paris' Symphony. We heard the 22-year-old Mozart taking advantage of being given access to a larger orchestra than he had previously known: heavy blocks of sound sat next to much more delicate passages. These quick-fire contrasts could have been a little more clearly drawn, as I missed the dynamic extremes that Mozart surely intended, but there was no faulting the dexterity of the strings in particular, as they navigated some intricate passagework.

Elsewhere in the programme we were offered characterful performances of Debussy's ‘Petite Suite', Vaughan Williams's ‘Fantasia on Greensleeves', and Butterworth's ‘Banks of Green Willow'. George Butterworth is one of the great might-have-beens of English music - his career was cruelly cut short in the trenches of the First World War. The orchestra here charted a seamless course from the simple pastoral opening, audibly growing in confidence as the music opened up to something much more passionate and involving. For me, this was the emotional high point of the evening.

On my way out I overhead one audience member suggesting that the standard of the playing would justify double the price she had paid for her ticket. High praise indeed - and we are fortunate in Haslemere to have such a talented and committed group on our doorstep.

Matthew O'Sullivan
