Events

The Society performs three concerts with the orchestra and chorus every year in the Haslemere Hall. An additional orchestral concert is held in St Christopher's Church, Weyhill, often featuring a soloist from within the orchestra.

December, HMS 90th Anniversary Concert

Saturday 7th December 2013, 19:30 Haslemere Hall (venue) Tickets: £8.00 - £15.00
Under 18: Half price
Conductor James Ross

Works:
Beethoven Symphony No. 3 Eroica
Bach Cantata No 140 Wachet auf (First movement)
Conducted by Christopher Hann
Vivaldi Gloria

Soloists:
Soprano Sara Wallander Ross
Mezzo-Soprano Angharad Lewis

For the celebratory concert on Saturday 7th December the Haslemere Musical Society is performing Beethoven's Symphony No 3 (Eroica). This work was performed at the first concert of the Society in 1923. At that time any shortages in the orchestra were filled in by piano support. Ninety years on the orchestra will perform in all its glory - no gaps. The chorus will join the orchestra for two wonderful choral pieces: the famous Vivaldi Gloria and the first movement of a Bach Cantata, guaranteed to delight, the Cantata No 140. James Ross is the conductor for all but the Bach which will be conducted by the Society's Chorus Master, Christopher Hann.

Latest Reviews

Haslemere Musical Society launch season with impressive orchestral concert
St Christopher's Church, Wey Hill, Haslemere, 19th October 2013

The Haslemere Musical Society, forever ambitious and resourceful, opened their season on Saturday 19th October when the Haslemere Symphony Orchestra performed to a packed St Christopher's Church.

These purely amateur musicians perform because they love doing so, and there are no auditions as members can soon discern whether they are up to standard. And up to standard they certainly were in ambitious works by the French composers Gabriel Fauré and Maurice Ravel. Known mainly for the famous Sicilienne, performed by flautists the world over, Fauré's suite taken from his incidental music for Maeterlinck's play Pélleas et Mélisande contains much haunting music reflecting the tragic nature of the drama. In this the oboe is prominent, and David Farnworth contributed brilliantly, partnered by some excellent flute, clarinet, and horn playing. The warm acoustics of the church gave the strings a wonderfully warm sound, which was also brought to bear in the following work. This was Maurice Ravel's Le Tombeau de Couperin, a tribute not only to eighteenth-century musicians and musical life, but to the composer's friends killed in the First World War. Antique dances are given a piquant twist here, with much imaginative orchestration, and the strings excelled themselves in the final Rigaudon. But in other places, such as the dissonant Forlane we could have wished for a little more flow and a little less stiltedness from the interpretation of conductor James Ross.

Johannes Brahms's D major Serenade is the work of a young man (not the stolid bearded figure of later years) still mourning his mentor Robert Schumann and not yet daring to emulate his hero Beethoven by writing a symphony (that was to come some fifteen years later). But the serenade, albeit modelled on Mozart's Posthorn Serenade, is symphonic in many ways, and in places sinks to tremendous emotional depths. Roger Doulton opened splendidly on the French horn, and indeed contributed wonderfully to the whole performance. There were glorious choirs of wind instruments and some lovely string playing.

Shelagh Godwin
